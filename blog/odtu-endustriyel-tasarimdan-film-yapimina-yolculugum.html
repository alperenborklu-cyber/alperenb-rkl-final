From METU Industrial Design to Filmmaking

By Alperen Börklü 5 Min Read

People ask me how an industrial designer becomes a filmmaker. My honest answer is: I'm not sure the transition ever fully happened. What actually occurred is that I discovered two fields were asking the same fundamental question from different angles — how do you shape an experience for another human being? Industrial design asks this question about physical objects and systems. Cinema asks it through time, image, and sound. The training is different. The essence is identical.

METU: A Design Education That Changed How I See

ODTÜ (Middle East Technical University) is one of the most rigorous design schools in Turkey, and its industrial design program pushes students into the intersection of engineering, ergonomics, aesthetics, and human behavior research. The department's approach is deeply systematic: you are taught to identify a problem, understand its human context, prototype rapidly, and test against real users. This process is not natural to someone who wants to simply make beautiful things — it requires a kind of intellectual humility, a subordination of personal taste to user need. That lesson has never left me.

Design Thinking as Visual Storytelling

What METU gave me that film school might not have is the habit of thinking in systems. A film, like a product, is a complex system: narrative, visual design, sound, performance, pacing, structure all interact. Industrial designers learn to see these interactions rather than isolating individual components. When I approach a film, I am naturally thinking about how each element serves the overall experiential intention — not just whether a shot is beautiful, but what it does to the audience's understanding at that precise moment.

The Pivot Toward Cinema

During my time at METU, I was already making short films on the side — not as a career plan, but because I needed to. Cinema felt like the only medium where I could simultaneously address visual design, spatial composition, human psychology, and narrative in a single work. After graduating, I chose to pursue web design and creative direction professionally (a natural extension of my design training) while developing my filmmaking practice in parallel. This dual track has shaped my entire approach: the commercial work funds the artistic work, and the artistic work keeps the commercial work honest.

What the Journey Has Taught Me

The most important thing I've learned crossing between design and cinema is that clarity of intention is more important than technical perfection. A film with a clear vision made with modest resources communicates more powerfully than a technically impeccable film without a point of view. My design training is obsessed with intention — why does this object exist? What is its purpose? Who is it for? Bringing that discipline to filmmaking keeps my work grounded, even when it risks being experimental. I always know what I'm trying to do and why. The rest is craft — and craft can be learned.
